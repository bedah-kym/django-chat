# CIPESA — Kenya Internet Freedom & Disinformation (2024–2025)

**What it is.** The Collaboration on International ICT Policy for East and Southern Africa — a regional policy and research organisation that publishes the annual *State of Internet Freedom in Africa* report (now in its 11th edition, 2024). One of the few sustained continental sources on the *infrastructure* layer of African disinformation: how the platforms, governments, regulations, and civil society interact.

**Why this is in here.** The other Kenya files are *content*-focused (what's being said, by whom). CIPESA is *infrastructure*-focused (what shapes what can be said, surveilled, moderated). Both lenses are required.

## The Kenya headline

> "Kenya's disinformation enterprise remains sophisticated, lucrative and largely funded by political actors that exploit divisions around ideological, ethnic, economic, and demographic lines while harnessing the power of social media."

Three words from this sentence are SIGNET-critical:
- **Sophisticated** — confirms what DFRLab observed about a *marketplace*.
- **Lucrative** — there's money in it. Kenyan disinformation has a P&L.
- **Largely funded by political actors** — domestic political establishment, not foreign meddling. Africa Center 2024 said 5 of 9 Kenya campaigns were domestic; CIPESA's framing implies the funding is mostly domestic even for the others.

## The four axes of exploitation

CIPESA names the wedges that operators exploit:
1. **Ideological** — political alignment / partisan division
2. **Ethnic** — the dominant axis (matching Nyabola)
3. **Economic** — class, livelihood anxiety, "you've been left behind" framing
4. **Demographic** — Gen Z vs older, urban vs rural, diaspora vs domestic

This is a more granular taxonomy than SIGNET's `identity_wedge` (which collapses all four into one tag). The CIPESA breakdown is worth keeping in mind when interpreting `identity_wedge` hits — *which* wedge is being exploited matters operationally.

## Kenya — infrastructure baseline (2024 numbers)

> "**22.7 million internet users**, of which **13 million are active on social media**, while cellular mobile connections stood at **66 million** in 2024."

Population context: ~57 million in Kenya. So:
- ~40% of Kenyans online.
- ~23% on social media — *not* a majority. The political class and engaged minority is overrepresented.
- ~115% mobile penetration (multiple SIMs per person).

Implication for SIGNET: **the social media discourse is not representative of Kenya.** It is the discourse of a politically engaged urban minority + diaspora. Conclusions drawn from r/Kenya about "what Kenyans think" should be heavily caveated — you are observing about a quarter of the country.

## Press freedom decline

> "Kenya's media rankings have declined, with the Media Council of Kenya reporting a total of **74 cases of press freedom violations in 2024**."

This matters because:
1. Press freedom decline correlates with disinformation effectiveness — less independent reporting = fewer corrections.
2. When SIGNET surfaces narratives that contradict government framing, those narratives may have been *suppressed in mainstream media* by independent reasons (legal action against journalists, advertising pressure, license threats). The *absence* in mainstream press isn't automatic evidence the narrative is fringe.

## FECOMO — multi-stakeholder coalition

> "Kenya's National Coalition on Freedom of Expression and Content Moderation (FECOMO) brings together more than 20 state, civil society, and media entities to ensure that content moderation protects freedom of expression while tackling harmful content."

FECOMO is an early-stage operational network. If SIGNET ever produces public outputs (alerts, reports, dashboards), FECOMO is the kind of body to engage with — they're the multi-stakeholder venue for translating findings into policy/platform action.

## CIPESA's 2024 State of Internet Freedom report — election focus

2024 was a "Year of Democracy" with 20+ African countries going to the polls. CIPESA's 11th-edition report focuses on the interplay between technology and elections — directly relevant prior reading before Kenya's 2027 election cycle starts heating up in 2026.

## What SIGNET should borrow

1. **Disaggregate `identity_wedge` into the four CIPESA axes** as a future enhancement. Worth a future emergent tag set: `wedge_ethnic`, `wedge_economic`, `wedge_demographic`, `wedge_ideological`. The current single tag is too coarse to drive nuanced action.
2. **Hold the "23% of population" caveat firmly.** Anything SIGNET says about Kenyan political discourse is about the *online* segment. Avoid generalising findings to "Kenyans believe X."
3. **The 74 press-freedom violations matters operationally.** If SIGNET surfaces a narrative that *should* be in mainstream press but isn't, ask whether suppression rather than fringe-ness is the explanation.
4. **FECOMO is a likely future partner** for human-eyes / cross-reference work — they're the venue.
