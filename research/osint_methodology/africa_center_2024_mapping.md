# Africa Center for Strategic Studies — *Mapping a Surge of Disinformation in Africa* (2024)

**What it is.** The single most data-dense recent open report on African disinformation. Published March 2024 by the Africa Center for Strategic Studies (a U.S. Department of Defense academic institution focused on African security). Aggregates campaigns documented across the continent during 2022–2023.

**Why this is the most important *numbers* source.** The other research in this folder is methodology and case work. This one gives you the *scale*. If you want a single source to cite when explaining what disinformation in Africa actually looks like by volume, this is it.

## Headline finding

> "**189 documented disinformation campaigns** in Africa in 2023 — nearly **quadruple the number reported in 2022**."

The growth rate is the story. Whatever the absolute number undercounts (and it does — only campaigns specifically *documented* are included), the *trajectory* is clear: large, accelerating, and now a structural feature of African political life rather than an episodic phenomenon.

## Real-world impact

> "Disinformation campaigns have directly driven deadly violence, promoted and validated military coups, cowed civil society members into silence, and served as smokescreens for corruption and exploitation."

This is the answer to "why does this matter." Disinformation in the African context is not primarily about elections — it's about whether soldiers obey orders during coups, whether journalists self-censor, whether ethnic violence escalates. The *stakes* are different than the Western "should we trust this news article" framing.

## Foreign actors (2023 data)

> "About **60% of campaigns** discovered in 2023 are attributed to **Russia, China, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Saudi Arabia, and Qatar**."

Breakdown:
- **Russia** — 80 documented campaigns across 22+ countries. ~40% of all African disinformation campaigns. By far the dominant single actor. Wagner Group / successor networks particularly active in Sahel.
- **China** — second-largest foreign source, focused on narrative shaping around BRI projects, Taiwan, and Western criticism.
- **UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar** — regional power competition surfacing as disinfo in the Horn of Africa especially.

## Kenya-specific findings

> "**Kenya experienced 9 disinformation campaigns**, with **5 being domestic**."

This is the single most calibrating number for SIGNET:
- **Domestic actors are the majority of Kenya's documented campaigns** — 5 of 9 (~56%). This matches Nyabola's historical frame.
- Foreign campaigns targeting Kenya are present but minority (4 of 9). Russia and China both active, plus likely UAE.
- The *absolute number* is low (9) — Kenya is not a high-volume target compared to Sahel countries, the Horn of Africa, or DRC. The campaigns that exist are *political*, not military/coup-related.

## Continental context — domestic vs foreign trend

> "Domestic actors have increasingly integrated disinformation into their political playbooks, notably during Kenya's 2022 and Nigeria's 2023 elections."

The growth in *domestic* campaigns is what makes this report's framing different from a few years prior — the Russia/Wagner story dominated 2020–2022 coverage; the 2024 data shows the *internalisation* of disinformation by African political establishments themselves. Cambridge Analytica's 2013/2017 Kenya work was an early signal of this; by 2023 it's the dominant pattern.

## What SIGNET should take from this

1. **Calibrate expectations: ~9 campaigns/year in Kenya, ~5 domestic.** If SIGNET's 30-day soak surfaces nothing resembling a campaign, that's plausibly correct — the rate is roughly one per six weeks. If SIGNET surfaces *dozens*, the tagger is over-firing.
2. **Domestic actors are the working assumption.** Stop thinking about disinfo in Kenya as "foreign meddling" — it's a Kenyan political-economy phenomenon, with foreign assistance occasionally layered on top. The `SignetAccount` tier system and the narrative-clustering should be sensitised to *domestic* actor patterns first.
3. **The 2023 → 2024 inflection.** 2024 is a "Year of Democracy" — 20+ African countries went to the polls. Numbers for 2024 (which would be in the next report cycle) will almost certainly be higher than 189. Plan for the soak to reveal patterns that haven't been published yet.
4. **The stakes framing matters when SIGNET surfaces a "high tier" finding.** What's at stake for a high-confidence narrative cluster isn't a takedown notice — it's whether ethnic violence escalates or whether a journalist self-censors. Action recommendations downstream of SIGNET need to be calibrated to those stakes.
